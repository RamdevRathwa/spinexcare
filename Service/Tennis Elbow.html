Understanding Tennis Elbow

Tennis elbow, medically known as lateral epicondylitis, is a painful condition affecting the tendons on the outside of the elbow

What is Tennis Elbow?

Tennis elbow is an overuse injury that occurs when the tendons connecting the forearm muscles to the outside of the elbow become inflamed or damaged. Despite its name, only about 5% of tennis elbow cases are actually related to playing tennis.

  • Extensor Tendon Inflammation - The extensor carpi radialis brevis tendon is most commonly affected, causing pain on the outer elbow
  • Repetitive Strain Injury - Caused by repetitive gripping, twisting, or lifting motions that stress the forearm tendons
  • Microtears in Tendons - Small tears develop in the tendon tissue from overuse, leading to pain and inflammation
  • Occupational Hazards - Common in professions requiring repetitive arm movements: carpenters, painters, butchers, and computer workers
  • Sports-Related - Tennis, golf, weight training, and other sports that involve repetitive forearm use
  • Age Factors - Most common in adults aged 30-50, as tendons lose elasticity with age

Early intervention is crucial to prevent tennis elbow from becoming a chronic condition that significantly impacts daily activities.

Recognizing Tennis Elbow Symptoms

Tennis elbow presents with distinctive symptoms that typically develop gradually

Outer Elbow Pain

Pain and tenderness on the bony bump on the outside of the elbow, which may radiate down the forearm.

Weak Grip Strength

Difficulty holding objects, weakness when shaking hands, or dropping items due to pain and muscle weakness.

Pain with Specific Movements

Pain worsens when shaking hands, turning doorknobs, lifting objects, or extending the wrist against resistance.

Morning Stiffness

Stiffness in the elbow and forearm upon waking, with pain that may improve with gentle movement throughout the day.

Preventing Tennis Elbow Recurrence

Proactive strategies to protect your elbows and maintain pain-free function

Forearm Strengthening

Regular eccentric and concentric exercises for forearm muscles to maintain tendon strength and resilience.

Proper Technique

Use proper form during sports and work activities. Avoid bending wrist during lifting and use whole arm movements.

Frequent Breaks

Take regular breaks during repetitive tasks. Follow the 20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, rest for 20 seconds.

Proper Equipment

Use ergonomic tools, properly sized sports equipment, and consider counterforce braces during high-risk activities.

Ice After Activity

Apply ice to the outer elbow after strenuous activity or at first sign of discomfort to prevent inflammation.

Gradual Progression

Slowly increase intensity of new activities or sports. Avoid sudden changes in training intensity or duration.
