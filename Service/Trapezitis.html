Understanding Trapezitis

Trapezitis is the inflammation of the trapezius muscle, one of the largest and most important muscles in the upper back and neck

What is Trapezitis?

The trapezius muscle is a large, triangular muscle that extends from the base of the skull to the mid-back and out to the shoulder blades. Trapezitis occurs when this muscle becomes inflamed, strained, or develops trigger points due to overuse, poor posture, or stress.

  • Upper Trapezius Strain - Affects the neck and shoulder area, commonly causing tension headaches and neck stiffness
  • Middle Trapezius Involvement - Causes pain between shoulder blades and upper back discomfort
  • Lower Trapezius Issues - Leads to mid-back pain and postural problems
  • Postural Stress - Prolonged sitting, computer work, and forward head posture strain the trapezius
  • Emotional Tension - Stress and anxiety cause involuntary trapezius muscle contraction and spasm
  • Acute Injury - Sudden movements, heavy lifting, or whiplash can traumatize the trapezius muscle

Trapezitis is often mistaken for other conditions, but proper diagnosis and targeted treatment can provide rapid relief.

Recognizing Trapezitis Symptoms

Trapezitis presents with distinctive symptoms that affect daily activities and quality of life

Upper Back & Neck Pain

Dull, aching pain or sharp, burning sensation in the upper trapezius area, often worse with movement or prolonged sitting.

Tension Headaches

Headaches originating from the base of the skull, radiating to temples and behind eyes due to muscle tension.

Shoulder Stiffness

Difficulty moving shoulders, especially when reaching overhead or behind back. Feeling of tightness and restricted motion.

Trigger Points & Knots

Palpable hard knots in the trapezius muscle that are tender to touch and may refer pain to other areas.

Preventing Trapezitis Recurrence

Daily habits and awareness strategies to keep your trapezius muscles healthy and pain-free

Proper Workstation Setup

Monitor at eye level, elbows supported at 90 degrees, and regular breaks to prevent trapezius strain.

Frequent Movement Breaks

Stand up and stretch every 30 minutes. Perform shoulder rolls and neck stretches throughout the day.

Stress Reduction

Practice deep breathing, meditation, or progressive muscle relaxation to reduce trapezius tension.

Balanced Exercise

Strengthen opposing muscle groups and maintain trapezius flexibility through regular stretching.

Phone Awareness

Avoid cradling phone between ear and shoulder. Use speakerphone or headphones for long calls.

Sleeping Position

Use supportive pillow that maintains neutral neck alignment. Avoid stomach sleeping.
